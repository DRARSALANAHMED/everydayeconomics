The curious economist

A few years ago, someone I'd just met asked me a simple question: "Why doesn't the government just give everyone money? Wouldn't that end poverty?" No malice in it, no politics — just a real question, asked by someone who lives in the real world and has every right to ask it.

I didn't have a five-second answer. And that bothered me more than it should have, because I'd spent over a decade teaching economics in a classroom, and somehow the classroom had never prepared me to answer that question for someone standing in front of me at a dinner table.

That's the gap this series lives in. Not a textbook. Not a lecture. Short stories — six or seven chapters, five minutes each, thirty pages total — built for people who want to think about money, prices, and policy, not memorize them. I'm not here to convert you to a school of thought. I'm here to help you ask better questions than the one I couldn't answer that day.
