Receipt Not Found

You pay taxes every day. Do you know what you bought?

Arsalan Ahmed

Before We Start

This book won't teach you economics the way a classroom does. No graphs, no models, no jargon. Instead, it gives you something simple and practical: the ability to see what is really happening behind a tax payment you make every single day without fully understanding where it goes.

Specifically, this book is about a receipt.

Not a receipt in the simple sense. You have seen tax printed on a shop receipt. You have seen it deducted on a pay slip. You have filed a tax return and signed your name at the bottom. Those traces exist. But nobody has ever assembled all of those traces into one clear, readable picture of what your money collectively bought once it left your hands.

That is the receipt this book is printing for you.

We are going to follow three people: Elin, Tane, and Naledi. They work in different sectors. None of them know each other. But together, their stories show both ends of the same transaction: the paying and the spending, the tax collected and what it actually became on the other side.

By the end of this book, you will be able to read a receipt that was never designed to be read by a single person. But it was always meant for you.

Let us begin.

Opening

You Already Paid For This

Naledi drove to the market on a Tuesday morning. The road was smooth, the traffic lights were working, and the street signs were clear. She did not think about any of this. She was thinking about the supplies she needed to buy for her guesthouse, and whether she had time to get back before her first guests checked in.

She had paid her quarterly tax bill the week before. She had thought about that. She had thought about it quite a lot, sitting at her kitchen table, adding up the numbers.

She did not connect the two things. Most people never do.

The pieces of your receipt exist everywhere around you. Nobody has ever assembled them into one place for you to read.

This book assembles them. Not perfectly. Not down to the last cent. But clearly enough that the next time you pay a tax, you will have some sense of what you are actually buying.

Chapter One

The Unread Receipt

Naledi's kitchen table, end of the month

Naledi runs a small guesthouse with six rooms. She has owned it for four years, built it up slowly, one good review at a time. At the end of every month, she sits at her kitchen table with a stack of papers in front of her.

Property tax. Business license renewal. A tourism levy she still does not fully understand, even after four years of paying it. Personal income tax. She adds them all up, writes the total at the bottom of her notepad, and stares at it for a moment.

She pays it, because she has to. But before she closes the folder and goes back to work, she sometimes wonders, briefly, where exactly it all goes. She cannot answer the question. Thus, she lets it go and gets on with preparing rooms for next week's guests.

She does not know that some fraction of what she just paid will, within months, become a stack of new textbooks in a classroom across town. She does not know that another fraction will go toward repairing a stretch of road that she uses twice a week on her supply runs. She does not know these things because nobody told her. The system was never designed to send her a personalised breakdown. But the connections are real. They are just invisible from where she is sitting.

Elin's classroom, the following term

Elin has been teaching at the same public primary school for nine years. She knows which students need more time, which ones learn better with pictures, and which ones just need someone to believe they can do it.

At the start of this term, a delivery arrived: new textbooks for her class. The first new set in three years. The old ones had torn covers and pages that had been scribbled over and rubbed out so many times the text was hard to read in places.

She spent the morning handing them out. She watched her students open the covers carefully, the way you handle something new. One boy ran his hand across the first page, feeling the clean paper. Elin smiled.

She did not think about where the money for those books came from. She had submitted the request through the school budget process, waited for approval, and now the books were here. That was the whole story, as far as she could see it.

The rest of the story, the tax payments, the pooled budget, the allocation, the procurement process, all of that happened somewhere she could not see. But it was real. And without it, the books would not have arrived.

Tane's work site, the same week

Tane has worked in road construction and repair for nine years. He does not think of himself as someone who works for the government. He thinks of himself as someone who fixes roads, which is what he does every day, with a crew he has worked alongside for the past four years.

This week they are repaving a stretch of road on the edge of town. Drivers have been complaining about it for months. There are three significant potholes and one section where the surface has cracked badly enough that it catches on vehicle tyres in wet weather.

Tane and his crew will spend four days on this stretch. They will mill the old surface, lay new asphalt, compact it, and mark the lanes. By the end of the week, it will look and feel like a different road.

His paycheck comes from his employer, a construction company that won a government contract, which was funded through a public works budget, which was built from tax revenue collected from businesses and individuals across the country, including small guesthouse owners who sit at kitchen tables at the end of the month wondering where their money goes.

Tane does not know Naledi. He will never meet her. But the road he is fixing this week is one she drives on twice a week. And a small, untraceable fraction of what she paid last month is, in some real sense, in the work he is doing right now.

What is really happening:

Naledi, Elin, and Tane are connected by a receipt that none of them have ever seen assembled in one place.

Naledi paid taxes whose destination she could not trace. That money, combined with contributions from thousands of other people and businesses, moved through a budget process and became many things at once: part of an education allocation that funded Elin's textbooks, part of a public works contract that funds Tane's wages, part of a dozen other allocations that will show up in other people's lives in other ways.

The system is too large and too shared for any single payment to be tracked to any single outcome. But this does not mean the payment disappeared. It means it became something too distributed to read at the level of one person sitting at one kitchen table.

The receipt exists. It is assembled at the level of the whole system. Nobody has ever printed it for you at the level of your own contribution. That is what this book is trying to do, imperfectly but honestly.

The Lesson

Taxes do not disappear. They become textbooks, roads, salaries, and services, spread across thousands of uses simultaneously. The pieces of your receipt exist everywhere around you. They were just never assembled into one readable document for you personally.

Chapter Two

Where Does It Actually Go?

If you could assemble your full tax receipt, what would the categories actually be? In most countries, government spending organises itself into a handful of large areas. Understanding these is the first step toward reading your own invisible receipt.

Public services and infrastructure. Roads, public transport, water systems, electricity grids, waste management. This is the most physically visible category when it works, and the most complained about when it does not. When Tane repaves a road, this is the budget line he is funded from. These projects are expensive, long-lasting, and benefit almost everyone, including people who never think about them until something breaks.

Education. Teacher salaries, school buildings, textbooks, university funding, vocational training. Elin's salary and the textbooks her students received this term both come from this category. Education spending levels vary widely across countries. In some, it represents a significant share of the national budget. In others, it remains lower than many economists and educators argue it should be. What is consistent is that where it is funded well, the effects show up in classrooms like Elin's, quietly and without announcement.

Healthcare. Public hospitals, clinics, medical staff salaries, equipment, and medicine. In countries with public healthcare systems, this is often one of the largest spending categories, partly because healthcare needs grow as populations age, and partly because medical technology continues to develop in ways that are effective but expensive. Healthcare spending tends to be less visible than infrastructure until you need it, at which point it becomes the most important line on the receipt.

Social protection. Pensions, unemployment support, disability assistance, subsidies for low-income families. This category exists to catch people during the most difficult periods of their lives: old age, sudden job loss, illness, poverty. It is often the most politically debated category, precisely because it involves a direct transfer from some people to others. But as the earlier books in this series explored, every society makes choices about how much of this kind of support it is willing to fund, and every choice involves a trade-off.

Security and public order. Police, courts, legal systems, emergency services, and in many countries military defence. This category funds the systems that make daily economic activity possible in the first place. Without basic security and rule of law, contracts cannot be enforced, businesses cannot operate, and the other categories on this list become much harder to fund or deliver.

Debt repayment. As explored in earlier books in this series, governments sometimes spend more than they collect, funding the difference through borrowing. That borrowing must eventually be repaid, with interest. Debt repayment is a well-discussed topic in policy and media circles, particularly during periods of high government debt. What most ordinary people rarely see clearly is how much of their current tax payment goes toward repaying past spending rather than funding anything new today. In some countries and some years, this is a significant portion of the national budget, quietly reducing what is available for the other categories on this list.

Your receipt is not one item. It is six, seven or even more major categories, each containing thousands of smaller uses, assembled at the level of an entire country.

This is why no single taxpayer can ever see their exact contribution land in one specific place. Naledi's payment did not become one specific textbook or one specific kilometre of road. It became a tiny, untraceable fraction of all of these categories combined, mixed with the contributions of millions of other people. The system was built to function at scale, not to report back to individuals. That is not a conspiracy. It is just how a shared system works.

The Lesson

Government spending falls into a handful of large categories: infrastructure, education, healthcare, social protection, security, and debt repayment. Your tax payment becomes a tiny fraction of all of these combined. The system was built for collective scale, not individual traceability. The money is real. Its destination is simply too distributed to read at the level of one person.

Chapter Three

The Many Small Receipts

Here is something that catches most people by surprise. You are not paying one tax. You are paying several, in different ways, at different times, to different levels of government, each funding something slightly different.

Naledi discovered this slowly, over four years of running her guesthouse. Each year, the stack of papers on her kitchen table grew slightly more familiar. But it never quite felt simple.

Naledi's full list

Property tax, paid because she owns the building her guesthouse operates in. This one goes to local government and funds the services closest to her front door: rubbish collection, street lighting, local road maintenance in her specific area. The streetlight outside her guesthouse, the one she relies on when guests arrive after dark, is funded from this line.

Business license fees, paid annually to legally operate her guesthouse. This funds the regulatory systems that issue permits, inspect premises, and maintain basic standards across the hospitality industry. The inspector who visited last year and checked her fire exits and kitchen safety standards was paid, in part, from this line.

A tourism levy, a small charge added to each guest's stay, collected by Naledi and passed on to the government. She spent two years not fully understanding what this one was for. Eventually she looked it up and found that it typically funds tourism infrastructure and regional promotion. The regional travel website that lists her guesthouse and brings her bookings from visitors who would not otherwise have found her draws its funding, in part, from this line.

Personal income tax paid on what she earns after business expenses. This feeds into the large national categories described in the previous section: education, healthcare, infrastructure, social protection, and the rest.

And then, quietly, invisibly, every time she buys cleaning supplies, breakfast ingredients, new bedsheets, or a replacement kettle for room three, a consumption tax is folded into the price. She pays it without a separate bill. It is just part of the number on the receipt, the one receipt she does actually receive.

The most visible tax bill is rarely the largest one. The invisible ones, folded into the price of everyday purchases, often add up to more.

This matters because the conversation about taxes is usually framed around the most visible payment, the quarterly bill, the annual return, the deduction on the pay slip. When people say their taxes are too high, they are usually thinking about that one visible number, while the consumption tax on their daily coffee, the levy folded into their utility bill, and the fee built into their vehicle registration are all quietly adding to the total without announcing themselves.

Understanding the full picture does not settle the question of whether taxes are too high or too low. But it does change which number you are actually arguing about. And arguing about the right number is the beginning of a more honest conversation.

The Lesson

Nobody pays just one tax. Property tax, business fees, income tax, tourism levies, and consumption tax all work differently, fund different things, and arrive through different channels. The visible tax bill is usually just the most obvious part of a much larger total.

Chapter Four

The Hard Question Nobody Likes

There is a question that surfaces in almost every serious conversation about taxation: should the rich pay more?

It sounds like a simple yes or no question. The more carefully you look at it, the more it resembles every other policy question in this series: a trade-off with real benefits on one side and real costs on the other.

Most tax systems already operate on what is called progressive taxation. This means that people who earn more pay a higher percentage of their income in tax, not just a higher absolute amount. The logic is straightforward: someone earning ten times the average income can contribute more without it threatening their ability to meet basic needs, while someone earning below the average cannot.

The argument for raising taxes on high earners further is built on this same logic. More revenue, drawn from those who can bear it most easily, can fund services that benefit everyone, including those who contribute relatively little because they have relatively little to give.

But here is where the conversation usually stops being simple.

When taxes on high earners or large businesses rise significantly, behaviour tends to change. Some investors put less money into new ventures, because a smaller portion of the return will end up in their hands. Some businesses and individuals move their assets, or even themselves, to places with lower tax rates, a pattern economists call capital flight. Some companies reduce hiring or slow expansion, because higher taxes on profits leave less available to reinvest in growth.

None of these responses are inevitable in every case. Their scale depends on how large the tax increase is, how mobile the people and capital involved actually are, and how the new revenue is used. A tax increase that funds education and infrastructure might attract more long-term investment than it discourages. A poorly designed one might do the opposite.

As with every policy explored in this series, the honest question is not simply whether to do something. It is what we are willing to accept on the other side of that decision.

In the first book of this series, we asked who pays for a policy. In this book, the same question applies. Who pays for a tax increase? Who benefits from the revenue it raises? And what changes in behaviour might reduce that revenue or create costs elsewhere in the system?

Different countries answer this question differently, based on their own economic structure, the mobility of their wealthiest citizens and businesses, and the specific services the additional revenue would fund. There is no universally correct answer. But there is a more honest way to have the conversation, one that holds both the benefit and the cost clearly in view at the same time.

The Lesson

Raising taxes on high earners can fund valuable services, but it is not without consequences. Investment, capital flight, and hiring decisions can all shift in response. The honest version of this debate, as with every policy question in this series, requires holding the benefit and the trade-off in view at the same time.

Chapter Five

Why The Receipt Feels Invisible

Think about the last time you drove on a smooth road. You probably did not think about it at all. You were thinking about where you were going, what you needed to do when you got there, what was on the radio. The road was just there, doing what roads do.

Now think about the last time you hit a pothole. You noticed that immediately. You may have said something out loud. You probably checked your tyres when you stopped. You likely remembered it the next time you drove the same route.

Tane's crew spent four days repairing a stretch of road that drivers had been complaining about for months. Once the repair was done, the complaints stopped. Not because people called in to say thank you. But because a smooth road asks nothing of you. It simply carries you forward, the way it was always supposed to.

Elin's students received new textbooks at the start of term. They opened them, wrote their names inside the cover, and got on with their lessons. None of them sat back and thought about the tax payments that funded those books. Why would they? The books were simply there.

Naledi herself walked past a working streetlight on her way home that same evening. She did not notice it. She noticed it three weeks later, when it stopped working, and the street felt darker than usual on her way back from a late check-in.

This is not ingratitude. It is simply how attention works. We tend to notice what goes wrong and overlook what is quietly going right. Public services are at their most invisible precisely when they are doing their job well.

We remember the pothole. We tend to forget the thousand smooth kilometres before it. This is why the cost of taxes often feels more visible than what they bought.

There is a second reason the receipt feels invisible: timing. The textbooks Elin received this term may have been funded partly by budget decisions made a year or two earlier. The road Tane repaired was funded by a contract approved long before this particular stretch became unbearable. Tax revenue collected today often funds things that will not be visible for months or years.

This delay breaks the simple, satisfying connection between paying and receiving that we feel when we buy something directly. With taxes, the payment is immediate and specific. The return arrives slowly, indirectly, spread across time and across many people at once. This does not make the return less real. It makes it harder to feel.

The Lesson

Public services tend to go unnoticed when they function well, and taxes fund things over long, delayed timelines. Together, these two facts make the connection between paying and receiving feel weak, even when the underlying value is real and constant.

Chapter Six

Three Questions For Your Own Receipt

You cannot print a perfect, personalised receipt for your taxes. The system is too large and too shared for that level of precision. But you can ask better questions, the same way the earlier books in this series gave you tools to question subsidies, policies, and prices.

1. Which category is this tax actually funding?

Before reacting to a tax change, ask which of the major categories it touches: infrastructure, education, healthcare, social protection, security, or debt repayment. A property tax increase affects different things than an income tax increase. A consumption tax affects different people than a corporate tax. Knowing the category tells you who is likely to feel the benefit, and over what kind of timeline.

2. Am I noticing the cost more than the benefit because the benefit is invisible?

Remember the smooth road and the pothole. A functioning service rarely draws attention. A failing one does. Before concluding that your taxes produce no return, ask honestly whether the roads you drive on are in good repair, whether the schools in your area have what they need, whether the emergency services work when they are called. These are your receipts, even if they were never printed that way.

3. What is the trade-off being proposed, not just the headline?

As with every policy in this series, the honest answer to any tax question involves a trade-off. More revenue for which service, at what cost to which behaviour? Less taxation for which group, at what cost to which public service? The headline rarely contains both sides of this. Your job is to look for the side that was left out.

These three questions will not give you a perfect receipt. But they will help you read the one that was always there, just never assembled for you.

Naledi still sighs at her tax total at the end of every month. But she now sighs at a number she understands slightly better than she did before. And understanding, even partial understanding, is the beginning of asking the right questions.

The Lesson

Which category is this funding? Am I noticing the cost more than the invisible benefit? What is the real trade off being proposed? Three questions that turn a vague frustration into something more honest and more useful.

Epilogue

Naledi Reads Her Receipt

A year has passed since Naledi first sat down to think seriously about where her taxes actually go.

She is at the same kitchen table, with the same stack of papers in front of her. The numbers have changed slightly. Some categories went up, one came down. She still adds them up, still writes the total at the bottom of her notepad, and still stares at it for a moment.

But then she does something she did not do last year. She goes through the stack slowly, one paper at a time.

Property tax. She thinks about the road repair crew she saw working two streets away last month. She thinks about the streetlight outside her front gate, the one her late-arriving guests always mention because it makes the entrance easy to find in the dark.

Business license fees. She thinks about the inspector who came through six months ago. She had been slightly nervous before the visit. But the inspector was straightforward, checked what needed checking, and left her with a clear report. One of her guests had asked recently whether the guesthouse had been inspected. She could say yes, with confidence.

The tourism levy. She had three bookings last season from people who found her through the regional travel website. One of them, a couple who stayed for five nights, left a review that brought in two more bookings the following month. She still does not know exactly how the levy connects to the website. But the connection is plausible, and the bookings were real.

Personal income tax. She thinks of Elin, though she does not know Elin's name. She thinks of a classroom somewhere in this city where a child is sitting with a textbook that has clean pages and a cover that has not yet been torn. She thinks of Tane, though she does not know his name either. She thinks of a road crew somewhere, doing the kind of work that makes the morning drive feel like nothing at all.

She still sighs at the total. But now she knows, roughly, what she is sighing about.

Across town, Elin is starting a new school year. Her students will eventually become taxpayers themselves, signing receipts they may or may not ever learn to read.

On the edge of town, Tane is preparing for another week of work. There is a new stretch of road on the schedule. He does not know who uses it most. He just knows it needs fixing, and that fixing it is what he does.

The receipt remains invisible to most people. Most of the time, it always will. But Naledi, at least, has learned to read the categories, even without seeing every individual line.

The next time your tax payment leaves your account, pause for a moment before you close the folder and move on.

You will probably never see the complete receipt. But you now know, roughly, what it bought. And roughly, it turns out, is enough to ask the right questions.

Before You Finish

Check Your Receipt

Before we close this book, here is a short check. Not a test. Just a way to see how much of the receipt you can now read on your own. Try to answer each question in your head before reading what follows.

Question one: Naledi pays property tax, business license fees, a tourism levy, personal income tax, and consumption tax built into her purchases. How many different kinds of taxes is this?

Five distinct kinds, each funding different things, from local services to large national programmes. Most people, like Naledi at the start of this book, are consciously aware of only one or two at a time.

Question two: Why could Naledi never connect her tax payment directly to Elin's new textbooks?

Because tax revenue from millions of people is pooled together into large budget categories before being spent. No single payment can be traced to any single outcome. The connection is real at the level of the system. It is invisible at the level of the individual.

Question three: Why do public services often feel invisible, even when tax money is clearly funding them?

Because functioning services tend to go unnoticed. We register what is broken far more readily than what is working. A smooth road, a classroom with adequate supplies, a streetlight that turns on at night: these things tend to disappear into the background of daily life precisely because they are doing what they are supposed to do.

Question four: Is raising taxes on high earners simply a switch that increases government revenue with no other effects?

No. While it can increase available funding, it can also influence investment decisions, capital movement, and hiring behaviour among those affected. Like every policy in this series, it is a trade-off: real benefits on one side, real responses on the other. The honest conversation holds both in view.

If you worked through most of these without looking back, you can now read a receipt that almost nobody around you has been shown how to read.
A Note From the Author

Why This Book Exists

Taxes are one of the most universally felt parts of economic life, and also one of the least understood in any complete sense. Not because people lack the intelligence to understand them. But because the system is genuinely too large and too shared for any individual receipt to ever capture it fully.

This book was written to give that invisible receipt some shape. Not a perfect accounting, since no such thing truly exists at the individual level, but a clear enough picture of the categories, the connections, and the reasons the link between paying and receiving feels so distant even when it is entirely real.

Elin, Tane, and Naledi do not work in finance ministries or tax offices. They work in the actual economy, at three different points in a system that quietly connects them every single day. Naledi's payment and Elin's textbooks are part of the same story. Naledi's payment and Tane's wages are part of the same story. The system just never printed that story for either of them to read.

If this book helped you see that story a little more clearly, it has done its job.

— Arsalan Ahmed
